Chapter 13


Our big opportunity came when Mordecai infiltrated a well-known startup out in Silicon Valley.

Wait, let me back up.

You see, the janitorial services angle only got us so far. A lot of companies had existing contracts, and we couldn’t get gigs with all of them. So my old pal Mordecai got creative. He’d read an old article from back in the late 90’s–the dot-com bubble days. The writer claimed to have just showed one day at a startup in SoMa. He claimed an empty desk and just started working. Crazy, right?

Yeah, well, we knew it wouldn’t fly at most companies. But we scoped out several–some of our hot targets–and analyzed their hiring practices. We found out the trick was to come in as an outside contractor or consultant. Oftentimes, one leg of a company will hire a consulting firm, whose consultants then have nearly free reign at the entire company.

DealsOn X was the high-flying, pre-IPO startup of the moment. Mordecai just showed up one day, dressed to the nines.

“Oh, you must be new here?” a woman asked him.

“Yeah, I’m a consultant with Archer Mendelson. Doing a little profit analysis for the new CRM rollout.”

“Oh, cool.”

And that was that. DealsOn was too concerned with making themselves look more profitable than they really were, to notice a guy like Mordecai roaming the halls.


From operations, Mordecai was able to infiltrate the finance arm. Much of it was outsourced anyway, so Mordecai just pretended to be an outside partner.

The moneyshot was buried within dealings the company had with its numerous partners. DealsOn X was using some dirty accounting tricks to book revenues that, technically, weren’t actually revenues. They were more like IOUs. But the partner contracts had opt-out clauses, which were being executed left and right. Meanwhile DealsOn was still posting the non-existent revenue to its bottom line. DealsOn X… more like, CrapOn X.

Mordecai gathered the proof that we needed to blow this IPO out of the water.

Then we waited.


The IPO price of DealsOn X was slated for $26 a share. But the ibank handling the deal wanted a guaranteed pop for their pre-IPO investors, so they priced it at a more modest $23.

I woke up at 5am that morning to check on the pre-IPO morning buzz. There at my battle station, I used four of the screens to monitor the current share price and trading volume. The rest of the screens were wired up through Milkmaid, awaiting trade orders. We couldn’t use options on this one because of the timing issues. We had to be able to get in and out, fast.

Also, I didn’t tell Leroy this, but I was hoping that this would be my final op of the mission. He wanted to keep going. Damn fool. He really believed he could be a billionaire and, you know, somehow not get caught.


The night before, I’d compiled the cache of DealsOn X dirt that Mordecai had ransacked. And boy, was it a doozy. The plan was to get in early and ride the stock to its peak, then short it before going public with the dirt. Bloggers, journalists, finance forums–we hit every angle to make sure that this turkey wouldn’t fly.

Insider trading is a funny thing, you know? Congress, as of this writing, is allowed to make insider trades. Your elected Senator, perhaps, sits on a defense committee. He can receive classified information in a briefing, then go home, call his broker … and profit from this classified information.

But you and I, as private citizens? If we do this, and get caught, we get arrested. Yeah, sounds fair to me.


Due to a surge of buys in pre-market trading, the share price of DealsOn X opened at $27. Not bad. We had already grossed a cool $2.5 million.

But this stock was on fire.

Within an hour of its open, it was trading at $46. Holy shit, this could be it.

I felt like I was back in 1999, re-living the dot-com boom.

By 3pm EST the stock was trading at $58 per share. My poker gut told me that we’d hit our peak. Day-traders and profit-takers were about to make a run.


I pulled the trigger.

We sold it all.

I waited for the trades to clear before checking the balance in our accounts. We’d been using margin, so we were able to leverage our now $430 million into almost $750 million in capital.

I checked the screens and ran a Milkmaid script to tally up the new account totals. I almost threw up a little bit in my mouth. The total read:

$480. Million. Dollars.


We were almost there.

I began entering short sell trades across all our accounts. It was time for DealsOn X to take a fall. A swan dive from the nosebleeds.

Once all the shorts were in play, I began firing off the incriminating evidence against DealsOn to an email database of bloggers and journalists.

The email read:


Dear [redacted],


I have been following your work for years. Startup Finance Times is one of my favorite resources for getting up to date news and information.


Through a source close to DealsOn X, I have come into possession of some alarming documents.


They seem to prove that DealsOn X has been overstating their revenues; they are actually losing thousands of partners monthly and are drowning in a sea of red.


I’ve posted the incriminating documents at the following website for your perusal:


http://[redacted]


Best,


Woodstein Bernward


The stock closed at $54 dollars a share. Not bad. Probably the biggest one-day IPO swing of the year.

Our little publicity wouldn’t make headlines until the following morning. Though, we could tell from after-hours trading that insiders were already shitting their pants.

My posts on several of the biggest finance forums had received over 700 replies and 20,000 views.
