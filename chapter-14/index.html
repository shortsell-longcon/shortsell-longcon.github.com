Chapter 14


DealsOn opened the following day at $48. By 10am EST, three major finance blogs had covered the leak. This was followed within the hour by blurbs from the WSJ, CNN Money and Yahoo! Finance. They even found sources within the company to confirm the details made public by the leak.

We had DealsOn X by the balls, and they knew it.

Oh, and we also just happened to do the public a service by making sure that the share price of DealsOn X more accurately reflects the true value of the firm. It was a true Win-Win, as they say.


I watched as the share price sank to $35, then $31. It hovered there for about an hour and a half, before making a slight rebound. Turns out someone over at a major finance news outlet was nothing more than a glorified puppet of the ibank handling the deal. The outlet claimed the charges were false, but quickly had to backpedal.

I blinked, and the price was at $28. Then $26. Then $24 and a half. The regular-hours trading day was almost over. Sure, the price could plummet even further the next day, but I couldn’t wait.

It dropped again, to $21. It was now below the IPO price. I could see the company founders in my mind. Their faces beet-red. Overturning foosball tables in the company lounge. Sweat pouring down their veiny foreheads as a steam bellowed from their earlobes. All Wile E. Coyote as we, the Roadrunner, laugh at their plight.

With trembling fingers, I pulled the trigger at last. Covering the short as they call it.

It took a while for all of the trades to clear. Covering our shorts seemed to firm up the price a bit. DealsOn fell to $18, but stopped and held steady at $19 and a half.

We cut it close–with thirty seconds left in active trading, Milkmaid informed me that the complete set of trades had cleared.


I leaned back and cracked open a beer. It was a rare moment of celebration. I told Milkmaid to calculate our new net worth. We had timed it perfectly. Sure, we’d probably left $2-3 million on the table, but I could live with that.

A few moments later, Milkmaid returned with her answer as I nearly spat out my beer.

I stared at the screen in shocked disbelief.

The balance: $-217,000

Negative.

Negative $217,000.

I typed in the script command from the console, and logged in to a few accounts by hand.

Yup. He’d done it. Mordecai. Leroy fucking Brown, if that was even his real name. Double-crossed by a con … and a thief

I phoned Charley, frantic.

“What the fuck, Charley? What did you do?”

“What? What are you talking about?”

“Did you help Mordecai lift the funds? It’s gone. Fucking all of it.”

“No! No! Of course not.”

“Did you setup any offshore accounts for him without my knowledge?”

“No, of course not. We had agreed to share all account info between the three of us.”

“Fuck. Fuck fuck fuck fuck fuck fuck fuck. He got it, Charley.”

“How much?”

“All of it, Charley. Fucking all of it.”

I was gonna tell him what our final take was, but realized that would be a mistake. By my own back-of-the-hand calculations, I estimated our final take to be somewhere north of $530 million dollars.

“I’ll call you back,” I said, slamming down the phone.
