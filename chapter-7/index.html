Chapter 7


Two weeks of this and I was spent. I felt like a zombie, or, Edward Norton’s character in Fight Club. Bloodshoot eyes, nodding off during meetings, leaving documents related to my extracurricular activities in the copy machine. My code checkins grew progressively more sparse; I could hear whispers around the office.

It had been a few days since I’d been back to the industrial services company. The crews were rotated from building to building, so you never knew when you’d end up back at a particular office. After knocking out my usual routine, I checked on the logger. To download the data, you just plug the USB end of the logger into an Android phone or similar device. I wrote a custom app that would suck a week’s worth of data down in about thirty seconds. After replacing the logger, I moved on to the next office.


That weekend, I finally had time to sift through the pilfered keystrokes. The file looked something like:


rjamison


frogger29


John,


Please get those TPS Reports to me by end of day.


Thanks,


- Roger Jamison


www.cnn.com


wsj.com


…


…

The first two bits of data are key–the username and password. With that, I was able to access his company email remotely.

Part of the problem–okay, the biggest problem–was filtering through the flood of data. I provisioned a couple servers out of the Netherlands to handle the incoming stream of email and corporate documents. Most of the data was useless, especially from the junior associates. But it was like a game of chess. The king–the Chief Financial Officer of the company. We had to wade through the pawns and bishops, but each capture brought us closer to pwning their king.


We got our first hit when a junior VP at the company fired off an email about upcoming layoffs. “Layoffs” was one of our hot-button search terms. That along with: earnings, merger, acquisition, etc. We used keywords, just like Google, to filter through gigabytes of otherwise meaningless data. Upon closer inspection, we discovered that the company would soon announce a round of 6,000 layoffs. Only, just earlier that day, the CEO was running his mouth on an earnings call about what great shape the firm was in. Bullshit.

It was no telco merger of the century, but our cash was running low and we had to make a play. We enabled margin trading on our offshore accounts. Buying on margin means that you use your own funds as a kind of collateral with which to make further trades. With $13k, leveraging on margin to the max, we’d have $26k with which to make trades. One thing to know about margin–it’s not exactly risk-free. If your positions go south, you may experience what’s known as a “margin call.” If it gets to that point, you’re fucked. Your brokerage firm is only willing to lend you money based upon the value of the underlying securities held in your account. If that value begins to drop, automated trading agents step in to make trades–oftentimes at fire sale prices.

But we were playing with an edge. Margin calls, we hoped, would be the least of our concerns.
