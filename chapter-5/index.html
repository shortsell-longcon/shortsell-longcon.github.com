Chapter 5


Leroy and I met up periodically over the coming months; my day job slowly sucking my will to live. I longed for my days as a greyhat, but I was afraid to quit. I’d grown complacent; resigned to collecting a paycheck for the rest of my life.

One day I stayed late at work. It was one of those high-rise office towers in mid-town. Couple hundred different tenants. I went down a few floors, just as the cleaning crews were arriving. I watched as they entered the offices and began vacuuming the floors, emptying the trash. On the way home, I picked up a twelve-pack of Amstel Light. Leroy was coming over to watch the Knicks game.

We cracked open a few beers and turned on the game, but Leroy wouldn’t shut up about insider trading.

“Have you heard of Ivan Boesky?” he said.

“Rings a bell. Insider trading scandal back in the eighties?”

“That’s the one. I’ve been doing some research on Wikipedia. Do you know how much he made?”

“Enlighten me.”

“Over two hundred.”

“Thousand?”

“No, million. But as part of his plea deal, he was only fined a hundred mil. Dude walked out of prison after three and a half years, a hundred million dollars sitting in his bank account!”

“I’d rather have my freedom than be a millionaire in Club Fed. I don’t particularly like jelly or syrup.”

“Just think about it, man, a hundred million dollars. I thought you guys were saying there’s a near foolproof way of making the trades, so they could never be traced back?”

“The problem is, you have to find a way to get the insider info, before you could ever make a trade.”

And then it clicked.

“The crews,” I mumbled, as a wry grin crept across my face.

“Crew teams?”

“No, not rowing teams. Cleaning crews.”

Leroy wasn’t following. He’d never worked in a big office tower.

“Think about it,” I said, “who has nearly unfettered access to every office building in Manhattan?”

Leroy’s eyes lit up.

“But don’t they lock down the computers at night? Shred important documents?”

“Of course, but those are just systems, right? Every system has a vulnerability.”

“Like what?”

“Well, hardware-based keystroke loggers, for one.”

“English please, Sir Wozniak.”

“So Roger the Accountant logs out of his machine and heads home for the night. That’s what IT tells him to do. When you walk away from your machine, even for five minutes–to get a cup of coffee–you’re supposed to lock it down. Of course, no one really follows that rule. But most people either have a screen-saver lock, or just logoff when they head home for the night.”

“Okay …”

“Well, none of those measures even matter if you have physical access to a machine. This is a never-ending debate among IT Sec. Some folks believe that machines should be impenetrable, even when a potential attacker has physical access. Hence, locking down the machine when you walk away for a second. Of course, whole new attack vectors open up if you have physical access to a box. Especially if you can come back, every few nights, and collect sifted data.”

“So what, you come back each night and download their megabytes?”

I laughed. Leroy is clueless about technology, so I pulled out a thumb-drive to demonstrate.

“Picture a device, half this size. The USB end plugs into the back of the computer, but the other end has an attachment for a keyboard. The keyboard plugs into the other end. This is what they call a man-in-the-middle attack.”

“So … it records everything they type?”

“Exactly. Only problem is, you’d need regular access to the machine, to swipe off the pilfered data.”

“I see.”

“But, of course … what’s the first thing they’re going to type? Their username and password. As soon as you’ve got that, you can probably start accessing their email and file servers remotely.”

“Shit, man. Who can get these loggers? You gotta be C-I-A or what?”

“No, of course not. Five bucks on eBay and you’re off to the races. This is kid stuff.”

I went on and on about other techniques that were possible. Methods of extracting information from people’s computers. Backdoors, trojans, rootkits, you name it.
