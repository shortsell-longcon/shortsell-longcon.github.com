Chapter 1


You might be wondering why I’m revealing my story. After all, I got away with it. A poor kid from the Bronx–but here I am, $250 million dollars richer.

Let me back up a bit.

This is Charley. He’s my roommate, or was, back at Harvard. Charley’s hunched over a textbook, knee-deep in financial equations. I have no idea what they mean. It’s like when he looks at my computer screen. The Matrix, he calls it.

But I learned a lot from Charley. Charley turned me on to trading. He was convinced that he could come up with a formula, or really, a formula of formulas, that could beat the market. This is the holy grail when it comes to trading. He said that certain hedge funds had figured out a system, but it only worked for a while. Then shit would go south, like when LTCM had to get bailed out for a couple hundred billion.

These are called Black Swan events.

Charley turned me on to micro-transactions. Trades that are executed via software alone. Meaning, no human was ever involved in the decision to buy or sell. Wait–that’s not exactly correct. A human was involved in the creation of the software, but once it’s let loose on the world, the program trades using a mind of its own.

By the time Maria Bartiwhoha is yapping about the latest news on CNBC, it’s too late for the average investor. High-frequency trading bots will have already wiped out much of the profit-taking opportunity. Johnny E-Trade, however savvy, doesn’t stand a chance. Even with the fastest, dedicated trading setup, by the time Johnny tries to react, it’s too late.


Wait, let me back up.

This is me. Hunched over a DOS terminal at the age of twelve. It was my first computer. Well, it was my dad’s. But I used it so much, he didn’t have time to use WordPerfect anymore. Dad got an upgrade; I got my first PC.

You know how Matt Damon told that girl in Good Will Hunting how he could just play? That was me when it came to hacking.

Here’s what you need to know:

Your computer is a system.

The network is a system.

The Internet, interwebs and all things in between are just systems.

Systems are governed by rules; but the rules are implemented by humans. And every human has flaws.

Your toaster, for example, is governed by a set of rules. If you know what to look for, you can exploit the rules–to find a vulnerability in the system.

You can take apart your toaster and figure out how it works by looking at the components. The thing is–most people are afraid to take apart their toaster.

I took apart and rebuilt my first PC over fifteen times until I could do it blindfolded. A motherboard here, CPU there, RAM here, PCI card there, et cetera. Do you want one piece of advice on getting better at technology? Well here it is: don’t be afraid to break things.

You know why your mom or grandma doesn’t know how to use the computer very well? She’s afraid she’s going to break it. And yeah, she probably has before, by installing some app that made her cursor look like a frog that danced. But that wasn’t the problem. The problem was the payload. The malware embedded in the frog-cursor app that made penis pill ads pop up whenever she clicked on the big blue E.


So Charley and I became best mates, of sorts. I’d show him the scripts and hacks I was working out. He’d try to explain the latest ideas in finance, gleaned mostly from Harvard’s top-notch research library. Or the bits he picked up on insider blogs and finance forums.

Wait, let me back up. I said “hacks” earlier. Back to this word again. It’s a shame, really. A hack is merely a clever solution to a problem. The term “hack” can also mean, of course, a shoddy worker. As I’m sure you’ve heard writers referred to as hacks. There are two ideas going on here. One: a hack gets the job done. Two: they may not do so in the most elegant or maintainable fashion. That’s the beauty of a hack. In the world of savvy techs, people like Bill Gates and Mark Zuckerberg are hackers. The Kevin Mitnicks of the world–the outlaws? Those are crackers. As in, they crack into systems–like one might a safe, or a bank vault.

So as you might have guessed, I got pretty good at hacking. If you’ve seen the movie The Social Network, there’s a scene where Zuck builds the first version of Facebook in a night. He wrote a script to scrape his classmates’ photos, then re-hosted them in a database. (His dorm room, by the way, was in the same building where I stayed freshman year.) The birth of Facebook is the quintessential hack.

For some reason, hacking scripts together for a legitimate purpose never held much appeal to me. I was what they call grey hat, meaning, my work wasn’t exactly black hat, as in sending out 100 million Nigerian Prince emails, but it wasn’t 100% kosher, either.


Back to Charley. Sophomore year, we needed to find a new place to live. Most people ended up in three or four-person suites. That’s when we met Mordecai. He was our third. Mordecai Johnson Randolph III, as he liked to be called.

This guy was some piece of work. I should know. I eventually became business partners with him. Mordecai was, how shall we say, instrumental to my financial success.

He was a natural showman with innate charisma. When we met him for coffee to discuss being our roommate, he showed up in a suit and tie. And no, he wasn’t just coming from a debate, although he was head of the debate team for a while. The funny thing was, I never saw Mordecai lift a book. He’d just sit there in our living room, watching TV in his sports coat and khakis. “Never know when someone important might drop by,” he’d say.

I’ll tell you all about Mordecai’s dirty little secret, but for now, suffice it to say that this guy rivaled Jack Kennedy when it came to looks, charm and charisma. Integrity? That’s another matter entirely.


Pretty soon Mordecai’s ears perked up whenever Charley and I were discussing something. A new trading algorithm. A clever hack. He loved hearing about my latest exploits. And the formulas that Charley kept concocting. I was a little wary of letting Mordecai into our inner circle, but I didn’t really have a choice. He was going to be our roommate for a year, and he’d just sit there on the couch, watching those movies of his on an endless loop.
