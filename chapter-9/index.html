Chapter 9


As much as I trusted Mr. “Leroy Brown,” if that were in fact his real name, I had now put my life, my wealth (however newly-acquired), and freedom in his hands. We were both looking at some serious group shower time if our actions ever came to light.

At the bar that night, I decided right then, right there, that it was no longer about the money. I was in it for the thrill. Sure–a big, fat payday, too, but only if the risk was justified. Leroy and I had long ago shook hands on a 50/50 split. We’d offered a 20% cut to Charley, but he refused, flat out. Instead we paid him like any other financial services provider.

We didn’t tell him what we were up to, not exactly. In exchange, we were planning a big royal Fuck You to the boys over at Moldmen Plaques.


Would you trust a crook with $27 million dollars? How bout two dollars? Yeah, I didn’t think so.

Yeah, Leroy was definitely in it for the money. And the thrill of it. More than anything, I think he just liked playing all those characters. He loved pushing it to the limit. Mordecai loved playing these British characters, so we had to get him a fake UK passport.

I asked Charley to probe his connections for a good, trustworthy lawyer who handles offshore banking. Someone in the Caymans. Switzerland, despite its reputation, is not the haven for privacy-seeking billionaires it used to be.

That’s when I met Sal. I won’t use his last name, for obvious reasons. But before I met the man, I had to take a few “necessary precautions.” I spent two weeks running a full background check on the guy. I checked everything. Forget public databases. Sure, I ran him through all those and he came up clean. Near-perfect credit score. Pays his bills on time. Married for twenty-seven years.

But I also hired a P.I. down in Grand Cayman. The dick tracked Sal–his every move–for a week. Guy’s clean as a whistle. We interviewed former business associates and clients. We even found an old college acquaintance of his from Rutgers, who spoke to us off the record. Sal had never touched a drop of liquor in his life. Never smoked a joint. Probably never even took a drag off a cigarette.

I flew down to meet Sal at his office in Grand Cayman. He didn’t know this, but in my briefcase was a freshly-withdrawn, birthday cake-size stack of benjies. One million dollars to be exact. We estimated Sal’s net worth to be a little over $250k. And I was about to change his life with a small taste of Fuck You Money … as they call it in HedgeFund Land.


Sal runs a one-man, boutique operation out of downtown Grand Cayman. His secretary is a large black woman by the name of Condoleeza. They’ve been a team for over fifteen years.

I met Sal on a muggy afternoon; just another day in the Caribbean. Sal’s shiny bald head glistened in the sweltering heat. The window AC unit gulped for life. Sal wore a suit, black Italian, but it had seen better days.

“Mr. [redacted], what I’m looking for, specifically, is someone to handle my affairs, in the event of my … how shall we say, early expiration.”

“Go on,” he said.

“What I would like to setup is a dead man’s switch. I will give you a codebook with monthly encryption keys. Each month you will receive a communiqu√© from me. It may contain instructions, or just a simple message, such as, ‘I am alive.’ You will decrypt my message and follow any instructions provided.”

“This might be a little beyond me, technology-wise …”

“Don’t worry about that. If you choose to represent me, I will show you how to use the codebook and encryption keys. Don’t worry–they were using these techniques on submarines in 1946. It’s a simple yet secure method of communication.”

“Alright, continue.”

“If three months elapse, without you hearing from me, you are to trigger the dead man’s switch. I shall give you the instructions ahead of time, in the event this occurs.”

“Which are?”

“I have said everything I’m willing to say at this point. If you agree to be my counsel, we will then be covered by attorney-client privilege and I can say more. Let me assure you–I will never ask you to do anything illegal. Not in this country, nor in the United States. I do ask that you never inquire about the origin of my funds. Is that clear?”

“Yes, as much as it can be. Mr. … what did you say your name was again?”

He was trying to catch me off guard. I had scheduled the appointment under a fake name, to protect my interests in case he balked.

“That’s not important, Sal.”

“Well, I will have to think about it.”

“I should let you know, this offer implodes as soon as I walk out that door.”

“Oh … I see,” he said.

“I figured you might think I’m full of shit, so I brought a down payment. That is, if you choose to represent me.”

I put the briefcase on his desk, opened it, and turned it to face him.

His eyes looked like giant balloons.

“That’s one million dollars in unmarked benjamins. Go ahead, touch them.”

Sal picked up a stack of the bills. He pulled one of the bills out and held it up to the light. He’d seen his share of benjies–real and fake.

“You’ve got a deal,” said Sal, sticking out his hand.

I reached across and shook it.

“Now, please empty the briefcase,” I said politely.

Sal pulled stack after stack of hundred-dollar bills out of the case. As the bottom drew closer, a portion of a black and white photograph could be seen.

He finished emptying the case. At the bottom: a black and white photograph of an attractive, middle-aged woman. She was shopping at a local strip mall.

It was time to stay strong. Play it cool. Show an epic poker face–like I was playing at the final table of the WSOP. I had to convince Sal that I was willing to carry out my … insurance policy. At the bottom of the briefcase were seven black and white photographs, snapped by my private investigator. It was Sal’s wife, at the mall. One of his kids, at the playground. Another one of his kids, playing in their yard.

Sal got the message.

“You protect my interests, I protect yours,” I said.
