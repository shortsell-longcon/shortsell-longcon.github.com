Chapter 22


Cray Wench had discovered my identity. I don’t know how he did it, but the feds had tracked down the address of my Upper West Side apartment. My landlord gave positive ID; that was that.

And thus I became the target of a worldwide manhunt. The USA Today headline read: “Ex-Harvard Hacker Implicated in Insider Trading Scheme.”

The media called us the “Harvard Bandits,” despite the fact that we hadn’t stolen a dime, technically. Wench had Charley on his list of suspected accomplices, but there wasn’t enough evidence to bring a grand jury.

Two days of hiding out in the woods and I was ready to make my escape. I won’t go into all the details, but I managed to secure the funds at a variety of locations. Public Storage units, mostly. I had to get out of the States, and fast.

But there was one thing I had to do before disappearing for a while: I had to say goodbye to my mom.


I know, I know. Dumb move. But I had to. She was my mom, and I was about to disappear for life. Sure I’d try to get ahold of her later, but I didn’t know how long I’d be on the run.

The feds would be watching her. Had probably even convinced her to wear a wire. But I didn’t care. I called her from a fresh burner.

“Ma?”

“[redacted], what have you done?”

“Ma. Look, ma, I can explain.”

“Explain? You can explain this? I don’t think so. My friends at the spa won’t let me hear the end of it, [redacted]!”

“I know, ma, I know.”

“Your face on every paper. How do you think that makes me feel? That I raised a con.”

“Well I haven’t been convicted, yet. You know–innocent until proven guilty? And the real con–you know who that is, ma.”

“You mean Lawrence? He seemed like such a nice young man when I met him at Harvard that time. How could you get yourself mixed up in something like this? How?”

“Look, ma, I know they’re listening in on this call. And I know they’ll try and stop us from meeting, but I just want to see you one last time. I have to take a little trip … after we meet.”

“Fine. My son. My only son. Of course I’ll meet with you, but–and they probably don’t want me saying this–you be careful, you hear me?”

“I hear you, ma. Look–I need you to meet me tomorrow at five p.m., Grand Central Station. Beneath the big American flag.”

“Oh–Okay. I’ll be there.” She coughed a few times while saying, “F-B-I will too, F-B-I will too.”

“Thanks, ma. I can look out for myself. I’ll see you there. I love you.”

“I love you too.”


For my final public appearance, it was time to get creative. I made a call to an old Harvard buddy who had a connection with Improv Everywhere. They’re a comedy troupe in NYC; famously known for their flash mob hijinks. You may have seen their No Pants Subway Ride or Grocery Store Musical videos on YouTube. They normally don’t take requests, but the founder liked my idea.

For my disguise, I wore a 1970’s-era suit, a trench coat, a top hat and sunglasses. Clutching a briefcase, I made my way across Grand Central, hugging the crowds. At five ‘til, I spotted my mom beneath the giant American flag.

I texted her: “walk thirty feet toward the throng of people.”

As she disappeared into the crowd, I followed from afar.

Deep in the throng of people, I saw her from behind. “Mom,” I said.

She turned. “What have you done?”

“I know, I know. I really screwed up this time.”

I didn’t have much time. Federal agents were swarming as we spoke.

“Ma, I just want to say. I love you. And that I’m sorry. I never should’ve got mixed up in all this … but, it’s too late now.”

“I know–son. I’m disappointed in you. But I’ll always love you, no matter what.”

It was my last chance, so I gave her a big hug. I dialed a number on my phone, but didn’t yet speak. Out of the corner of my eye, I saw a man with a white earpiece, whispering something into his shirt sleeve. He lunged for me, but I ducked out of the way, yelling “Now!” into my cell.

The sound of the Village People’s YMCA boomed to life. A dozen boomboxes were tuned to a custom FM channel. An iPod playing the Village People was piped into an FM transmitter. The range was about fifty feet.

Suddenly, the throng of people around us threw off their overcoats to reveal Village People outfits. There were about thirty dancers in all–the Improv Everywhere ensemble. Each character was well-represented–The Cowboy, The Cop, The Construction Worker, The Indian, The Bear (for lack of a better word) and The Sailor. The mob spread out, dancing in formation.

Meanwhile, another group of people–about fifteen in all–donned large brown afro wigs and stunner shades. They circled the dancers and worked the crowd like so many hype men.

I ducked down, stripped off my trench coat and donned a large afro wig and shades.

Pandemonium unfurled, as you can imagine. Five agents rushed into the crowd of dancers. Traffic ground to a halt as hundreds of onlookers gathered around.

I made my way into the crowd, dancing and clapping like the other hype men.
