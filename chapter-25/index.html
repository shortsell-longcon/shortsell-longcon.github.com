Chapter 25


Sal was ecstatic when the $5 million dollar suitcase was delivered to his office. He snuck home and counted it in secret. Took him nearly two days to get a full count.

Decoding messages from a client seemed like spy stuff, but it was all worth it in the end. However, after approximately ninety-five days without contact, Sal feared for the worst. An arrest? Possibly, but it hadn’t made the news. Sal feared his client was resting at the bottom of the Atlantic somewhere. Or in a landfill in Rio.

Ever faithful, Sal triggered the dead man’s switch and delivered the following letter, anonymously, to news outlets and bloggers across the country:

My client was one of the so-called “Harvard Bandits.” To prove the veracity of these claims, an Amazon AWS key is included in this communiqu√©. It can be used to access a cache of documents obtained during my client’s activities. Some of which show dubious and possibly criminal activity on the part of several major financial institutions.

My client would like you to know that he never spent any of the funds obtained through these activities. Yes, he borrowed some, but paid it back. He has been living solely off of accrued interest. My client has arranged for $50 million USD of the funds to be donated, anonymously, in increments ranging from $500 to $50,000. Over the course of the next five years, these funds will be dispersed to over one hundred different non-profit and aid organizations.

The remaining $200 million USD–my client has hidden–in various public spaces, all across the United States and abroad.

Hidden in national forests. Buried in city parks. Sometimes, hiding in plain sight. The treasure is a mixture of solid gold bullion, gold coins, and laundered, untraceable $20s and $10s. My client’s last wish and bequest is for a global treasure hunt to commence. To aid in the discovery of said funds, my client has uploaded images, anonymously, which contain clues to the whereabouts of the hidden treasure. Maps, partial GPS coordinates, riddles and cyphers.

Using the process of steganography, my client has embedded these clues into various images and uploaded them online. What kind of images, you ask? Why pictures of cats, of course. Yes–our furry feline friends. Steganography works by encoding hidden messages into common image files–JPEGs, PNGs, GIFs–such that the original image appears unchanged (to the naked eye). Hidden, buried within the pixels? The keys to unlocking a $200 million dollar global treasure hunt.

From my client, good luck and best wishes.


Disclaimer: none of what you just read is true. Not even a smidge. So long and thanks for all the bacon.
