Chapter 18


Armed with Mordecai’s list of aliases, Jimmy went to work on locating our man. The third hotel he tried was the downtown Hyatt Regency.

“Hello,” said Jimmy, “can you patch me through to Kensington Danforth? It’s urgent. He’s staying in room 712.”

“Sure, just a second.” A beat. “Sir, our records indicate he’s staying in room 420. Perhaps he’s changed rooms since you’ve last spoken with him. Here, I’ll patch you through.”

Got him.

Jimmy setup a surveillance operation at the Hyatt; meanwhile I posted up outside Arkansas National Bank.

After a day of that, I grew restless. After a trip to the local spy shop, I was in business. Posing as an electrician, I installed two spy cams across the street from the bank. One above a coffee shop, the other beneath a neon “Diner” sign. With a lens the size of a button, the cameras were nearly invisible to the naked eye. They beamed a live feed to a nearby 3G modem. From there the data was sent directly to my server farm in Amsterdam.

I sat back, turned on HBO (quite a perk for the cheap motel), watched, and waited.


That night, Leroy showed up at the hotel, wasted. “Yeah, he’s with some broad,” said Jimmy. “Quite the looker. She’s got those uh … I mean no disrespect, but whaddya call those–hooker boots? The black leather thigh-highs.”

“Yeah, that sounds like Leroy alright. There’s one thing you should know,” I said to Jimmy, “Leroy’s quite the fan of the ladies, and he doesn’t seem to mind paying for it, if ya know what I mean.”

“Loud and clear.”

The next day, I get a text from Jimmy: “Bishop has left the palace.”

A few hours later I’m watching the live feed when Jimmy pulls up in a Lincoln Town Car. He’s dressed to the nines in a pinstripe suit. Two women get out of the car with him. “Bishop is in the pocket,” I text Jimmy.

He’d lost the tail at the airport parking lot, so it was a good thing we had the live feed.

From what emerged of the case later, we know that Leroy had opened the bank account under the name “ATM Distributors Global, Inc.” There was a major ATM distributor by the same name, only, without the Global. These are the people who made a fortune by stocking every mini-part, strip joint and fast-food shop with an ATM.

Leroy’s “assistants” turned out to be high-end escorts. Some $1000 dollar an hour agency that flies in girls from London, Tokyo and Paris. Apparently, the branch manager wanted to meet the man behind such a large cash infusion.

“Our business is very cash-intensive, as you can imagine,” Leroy told the manager. “We’ve blanketed the Northeast with our machines, but see a huge opportunity for us to expand in the South. Please, don’t mention this to the press, but we are about to acquire one of our major competitors in the Southeast. Hence the infusion of capital in this market.”

“Of course, Mr. Danforth. And we are glad to have your business. Thank you for meeting me in person. Is there anything else we can help you with today?”

Mordecai looked to his assistants. “The withdrawals,” one said.

“Ah yes,” said Mordecai, “we will begin making withdrawals next week. Does Tuesday work for you?”

“Of course. How much are we talking about?”

“Twenty-five million.”

The branch manager suddenly found it difficult to swallow. But he did so anyway.

“That’s quite the sum.”

“Our southern operations are doing quite well of late. An ever-increasing number of small businesses are opting to accept cash only. Our machines go in the corner. Customers get their cash. Everyone is happy, just about.”

“I see.” It seemed to make sense to the manager. And how could he explain turning down hundreds of millions in business to his boss?

“We’ll need an armored car to transport the funds to our secure holding facility. Will there be a charge for that?”

“No, Mr. Danforth. Let me check with my boss, but I believe we’d be happy to provide that service to a client such as yourself.”

“Great. I believe that’s all.” Leroy turned to his assistants. “Shall we?”

“It’s a pleasure doing business with you,” said the branch manager.

“And you, too.”


While Mordecai was doing business at the bank, I broke into his hotel room. It was simple, really. Using a magnetic card writer and cracked database of common key formats, I cloned Mordecai’s room key.

The lamp fixture provided some camouflage for a hidden mic. Off in the corner of the room, I installed a spy cam. By the time I ducked out to my rental car, the feed was live.


The next morning, after a night of carnal activities with his “assistants,” Mordecai made a call. The mic audio was kind of fuzzy. All I heard was: “Yeah, this is Kensington … ATM Distributors … I’ll be … an hour … 5150 Oa… I’ll see you there.”

Jimmy lost the tale on Mordecai’s red Corvette convertible. Mordecai had this habit of driving out to the airport and swapping cars, before engaging in illicit activities. Personally, I think he got the idea from that scene in Goodfellas. The one where Ray Liotta’s character is driving around town everywhere while trying to make meatballs. He thinks he’s just paranoid, but it turns out he really is being followed by helicopters.

All I had to go on was “5150 Oa”–not much, really. Google Maps to the rescue. The auto-complete feature narrowed down the address to one of two options: Oak Avenue and Oak Dale Drive. Jimmy took the Avenue while I scoped out the Drive.

Thirty minutes later I got a text from Jimmy. “It’s 5150 Oak Avenue. 100%.” I made my way over.

Mordecai had really outdone himself this time. It was a large warehouse in an industrial district. “ATM Distributors Global, Inc.” read the freshly-painted sign.

I had a sinking feeling in my stomach. Leroy had really stepped up his game. For a moment, I considered turning states evidence and narcing out Mordecai. Or phoning in an anonymous tip to the Feds.

Just then though, three large trucks pulled up to the address. Jimmy and I watched from down the black. Mordecai directed traffic as the construction workers carried large steel sheets into the building.

The fucker. He was construction a vault.
